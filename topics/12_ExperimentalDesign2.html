Experimental Design: General Info

Experimental Design: A structured approach to planning experiments

Goal: Maximize information while minimizing resources

Focus: Identify key factors and their interactions

Key Benefits:

Improves efficiency and reliability of results

Provides insights into cause-and-effect relationships

Two main Tasks in Experimental Design

1: Method optimization

Focus: Identify the best combination of factors (dynamically exploring design space)

Goal: Maximize performance (e.g., sensitivity, efficiency)

Example: Optimizing pH, temperature, and time for a chemical reaction

2: Determination of effects

Focus: Quantify the impact of individual factors and their interactions (statically exploring design space)

Goal: Understand cause-and-effect relationships

Example: Assessing how pH, stirring speed, and dosage influence pollutant removal

Introduction to Design of Experiments (DoE)

What is DoE?

A systematic approach to plan, conduct, and analyze experiments to understand the influence of factors on outcomes.

Goals of DoE

Identify key factors affecting outcomes and optimize processes efficiently.

Minimize experiments while maximizing information gained.

Ensure consistent and robust results under varying conditions.

Key Benefit

Reveals interaction effects and saves time compared to traditional methods.

Key Elements of Experimental Design

Core Components of DoE

Factors: Variables that are controlled or manipulated (e.g., temperature, pressure).

Levels: Specific values or settings of the factors (e.g., 50°C, 100°C).

Response: The outcome measured to evaluate the effect of the factors (e.g., yield, purity).

Design Structure

Full Factorial: Tests all combinations of factors and levels.

Fractional Factorial: Tests a subset of combinations to save resources.

Randomization: Ensures unbiased results by random order of experiments.

Why Structure Matters

A well-structured DoE ensures reliable, reproducible, and meaningful results.

Advantages of Design of Experiments

Efficient Experimentation

Reduces the number of experiments needed while extracting maximum information.

Identifies Key Insights

Reveals main effects and interactions between factors that are often missed in traditional methods.

Improves Process Understanding

Helps in optimizing and stabilizing processes for consistent performance.

Saves Time and Resources

Efficiently uses limited resources to achieve meaningful results faster.

Supports Decision Making

Provides data-driven insights to make informed and confident decisions.

Types of Experimental Designs

Full Factorial Design

Explores all possible combinations of factors and levels, providing detailed insights.

Fractional Factorial Design

Tests a subset of combinations to reduce experimental effort while maintaining key information.

Response Surface Methodology (RSM)

Used for optimization, focusing on the relationship between factors and responses.

Taguchi Design

Focuses on robust design by reducing variability and optimizing performance under different conditions.

Screening Design

Identifies the most significant factors with minimal experiments, often used as a first step.
